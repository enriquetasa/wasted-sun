{% trans %}This waste increases energy pricing for all{% endtrans %}

{% trans %} On sunny hours the Spanish peninsular grid often produces more solar than the local grid can absorb. That energy may go unused while other (more polluting, more expensive) sources remain in the mix: so the current system setup makes the consumer pay more. It is measurable, costly, and largely avoidable with better infrastructure and market design. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} What would help? Stronger grids and increased storage (that's batteries!) over time, and price signals that reward using clean power when it is abundant (during solar hours). That reduces waste, eases bills, and puts scarce clean generation to work—policy choices we already understand. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %}Methodology{% endtrans %}

{% trans %} Quarter-hourly unused solar (MWh) is derived from Red Eléctrica de España (REE) data, processed in a separate pipeline and stored in this project's database: each row is keyed by date with 15-minute segments of electricity wasted. Multiple rows for the same day are summed bucket-wise before display. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} The chart aggregates those quarter-hours into 24 hourly bars (Madrid calendar day). Any slots beyond the first 96 are folded into the last hour of the day. Day totals are the sum of all quarter-hour values. “So far this year” uses SUM(total_mwh) across rows from 1st January through the selected date. The average “per hour” figure is the mean of the 24 hourly bars (equivalently the day total divided by 24). {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} Euro amounts apply a simple illustrative rate to energy, not a market settlement—but they make clear that unused MWh are not economically neutral. {% endtrans %}
