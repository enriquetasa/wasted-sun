{% trans %}Why on earth are we paying twice for the same electrons?{% endtrans %}

{% trans %} On bright days the peninsula often makes more solar than the local grid can swallow. Instead of using every electron we grew for free, we leave it on the table while other kit keeps running— and households foot the bill for flexibility twice. Politely put: that should embarrass us. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} What would actually help? Grids and storage we stop talking about and start building, plus price signals that pay people to use clean power when the sun is generous. Less waste, lower bills, fewer excuses. We know what to do. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %}Methodology{% endtrans %}

{% trans %} The waste you see is real enough to measure—which is the infuriating part. Quarter-hourly unused solar (MWh) comes from Red Eléctrica de España (REE) data, then through a separate pipeline into this project’s database: rows keyed by date_day with qh_1_mwh through qh_100_mwh (15-minute buckets). Same calendar day, multiple rows: we sum buckets before showing anything. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} The chart aggregates those quarter-hours into 24 hourly bars (Europe/Madrid calendar day). Any slots beyond the first 96 are folded into the last hour of the day. Day totals are the sum of all quarter-hour values. “So far this year” uses SUM(total_mwh) across rows from 1 January through the selected date. The average “per hour” figure is the mean of the 24 hourly bars (equivalently the day total divided by 24). {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} Euro figures only appear if WASTED_SUN_EUR_PER_MWH is set: a blunt illustrative rate × energy, not a market invoice—still useful for remembering this isn’t “free” in anyone’s pocket. {% endtrans %}
