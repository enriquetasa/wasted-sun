{% trans %}Why pay for the same electricity twice?{% endtrans %}

{% trans %} On sunny hours, the peninsula sometimes produces more solar power than the grid can absorb locally. That energy may go unused while other plants stay in the mix—so consumers can end up paying for flexibility and constraints twice over. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} What helps? Stronger grids and storage over time—and market signals that reward using clean power when it is abundant, which can reduce waste and ease bills. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %}Methodology{% endtrans %}

{% trans %} Quarter-hourly unused solar energy (MWh) is derived from Red Eléctrica de España (REE) data. It is processed in a separate pipeline and stored in this project’s database before it is shown here: each row is keyed by date_day with columns qh_1_mwh through qh_100_mwh (15-minute buckets). Multiple rows for the same day are summed bucket-wise before display. {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} The chart aggregates those quarter-hours into 24 hourly bars (Europe/Madrid calendar day). Any slots beyond the first 96 are folded into the last hour of the day. Day totals are the sum of all quarter-hour values. “So far this year” uses SUM(total_mwh) across rows from 1 January through the selected date. The average “per hour” figure is the mean of the 24 hourly bars (equivalently the day total divided by 24). {% endtrans %}

{% trans %} Euro amounts appear only when WASTED_SUN_EUR_PER_MWH is set; they are a simple illustrative rate times energy, not a market settlement. {% endtrans %}
